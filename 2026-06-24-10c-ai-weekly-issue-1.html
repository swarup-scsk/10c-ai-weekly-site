Issue #1 · Wednesday, 24 June 2026

This issue covers enterprise AI deployments in manufacturing, the quantified cost of inaction on AI, new regulatory obligations in Europe and the UK, and two new tools for industrial automation.

TL;DR

  1. Samsung rolled out ChatGPT Enterprise and Codex to its entire South Korean workforce, marking one of the largest enterprise AI deployments in manufacturing. See Headline of the week.
  2. Thomson Reuters found that up to $143 billion in client revenue is at risk across legal, tax, audit and risk professions due to a gap between AI ambition and implementation. See Headline of the week.
  3. Eurocommerce asked the EU to exempt AI-generated ads from upcoming transparency rules, and the UK Data (Use and Access) Act complaints framework came into force. See Regulation and governance in The Briefs.
  4. Bloomberg reported that European manufacturers are accelerating AI adoption to fill a workforce gap from retirements, and two new products (Visteon D6Sigma and Intrinsic Intelligence) offer practical edge AI and robot programming solutions. See Enterprise and industry and Models and tools in The Briefs.

Headline of the week

Enterprise AI is moving from pilot projects to organisation-wide rollouts, and the financial penalty for hesitation is being named. Samsung’s deployment proves that a large manufacturing firm is treating generative AI as a core platform, while Thomson Reuters data gives consultants a concrete number to use with sceptical clients.

Samsung Rolls Out OpenAI Tools to Workforce in Major Enterprise Push A deployment of ChatGPT Enterprise and Codex to Samsung’s entire South Korean workforce and select international divisions, reported by PYMNTS.com (22 June 2026). Source: https://www.pymnts.com/news/artificial-intelligence/2026/samsung-rolls-out-openai-tools-to-workforce-in-major-enterprise-push/

10C take: This is a reference case for any European industrial client considering enterprise-wide AI. Samsung is a global manufacturing giant, not a software company. The decision to deploy widely, not in isolated pockets, signals that generative AI is now viewed as core infrastructure. When your client says “we are not ready for company-wide AI,” you can point to a competitor’s scale move and discuss the operational and governance choices that enabled it.

AI is Ready but Firms are Not: How Falling Behind on AI Implementation is Costing Clients and Talent A Thomson Reuters press release (22 June 2026) summarising its 2026 Future of Professionals report, based on a global survey of 1,800 professionals. Source: https://www.thomsonreuters.com/en/press-releases/2026/june/ai-is-ready-but-firms-are-not-how-falling-behind-on-ai-implementation-is-costing-clients-and-talent

10C take: The figure of up to $143 billion in client revenue at risk is a powerful conversation opener. The report focuses on legal, tax, audit and risk professions, but the warning applies to any knowledge-intensive professional service. For our clients in financial services and other regulated sectors, this provides a data-backed argument for moving from planning to execution. The risk is not just competitive disadvantage but direct revenue loss.

The Briefs

Short explanations of the other developments worth knowing this week.

Regulation and governance

Eurocommerce, the European retail association whose members include Amazon, H&M, Inditex and Ikea, asked EU tech chief Henna Virkkunen to exempt AI-generated advertisements from upcoming transparency rules under the EU AI Act. The request was reported by Reuters on 19 June 2026 (source: https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/ai-generated-ads-should-be-exempt-eu-transparency-rules-retail-association-says-2026-06-19/). Why it matters to us: This lobbying effort shows that major retailers view the AI Act’s transparency obligations as a significant operational concern. Consultants advising consumer-facing enterprises should monitor how this exemption request is handled, because it will affect compliance roadmaps for AI in marketing and customer engagement.

The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 Complaints Framework came into force on 19 June 2026, requiring all organisations in the UK to have a formal process for handling data protection complaints before they can be escalated to the Information Commissioner’s Office. Kennedys Law LLP published a briefing note on 23 June 2026 (source: https://www.kennedyslaw.com/en/thought-leadership/article/2026/dua-act-2025-complaints-framework-briefing-note/). Why it matters to us: Every enterprise handling UK personal data now has a new compliance obligation. For clients with UK operations, data governance projects must include a documented complaints framework. This is a concrete item to add to any regulatory risk assessment.

Enterprise and industry

Bloomberg reported on 18 June 2026 that European manufacturers, facing a wave of retirements, are accelerating AI adoption on factory floors as a strategic move to fill the coming labour gap (source: https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-06-18/europe-wants-ai-in-manufacturing-before-its-workforce-retires). Why it matters to us: This frames AI in manufacturing not as a choice but as a demographic necessity. When advising clients on Industry 4.0 investment timing, you can reference this macro trend. The urgency to act is reinforced by the workforce data, not just technology trends.

Models and tools

Visteon introduced D6Sigma, an edge AI product line for industrial automation developed with Qualcomm, which converts multi-camera video into real-time actionable events to improve quality, uptime and safety on factory floors. The announcement was distributed via press release on 18 June 2026 (source: https://www.stocktitan.net/news/VC/visteon-introduces-d6sigma-a-new-edge-ai-product-line-for-industrial-vlgg3o2k05tk.html). Why it matters to us: This is a commercially available, vendor-supported edge AI solution for industrial vision. For manufacturing clients evaluating automation options, D6Sigma provides a tangible reference point. Consultants can use it to benchmark other offerings and discuss real-time inference at the edge.

Intrinsic, an Alphabet company, launched its Intrinsic Intelligence platform at Automate 2026, enabling users to replace complex robot programming with drag-and-drop automation using AI. The launch was covered by The Robot Report on 22 June 2026 (source: https://www.therobotreport.com/how-intrinsic-eliminates-manual-robot-coding/). Why it matters to us: By lowering the skill barrier for industrial robot programming, this platform could reduce implementation costs and project timelines. For clients in manufacturing and logistics, this is a tool to watch. It may change the economics of automation projects and shift the skills required on the factory floor.

Client lens

This week’s developments converge most strongly on manufacturing clients. The Samsung deployment, the Bloomberg demographic story, the Visteon edge AI product, and the Intrinsic robot programming platform all point to a single conclusion: the tools are available, the workforce need is urgent, and leading firms are already moving. For clients in energy and logistics, the Intrinsic platform and the Visteon product are also directly relevant for warehouse automation and quality inspection.

For financial services clients, the Thomson Reuters report provides a compelling argument for accelerating AI adoption in knowledge work. The quantified revenue risk (up to $143 billion) can be adapted to their specific context. The two regulatory developments (Eurocommerce exemption request and UK Data Act complaints framework) affect any consumer-facing or UK-operating client, regardless of sector. Compliance advisors should flag the new UK framework immediately.

Conversation Starter

A point you can raise in a client conversation this week:

"A major manufacturer has just deployed generative AI to its entire workforce. A new report quantifies the revenue risk of not implementing AI at up to $143 billion. If your organisation is still in pilot mode, we should discuss what is needed to move to production scale."

This opens a useful conversation because it combines a real-world reference case with a specific financial argument, making it harder for internal decision-makers to defer action.
